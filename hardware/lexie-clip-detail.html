Lexie — Spring Jaw Clip: Detailed Design

Spring jaw · obstruction · L-bar layout · PETG · WX-024 · 2026-05-03

1 — How it works: the three parts

The three parts

Upper jaw (green)

A flat tab that reaches OVER the top of the book and rests on the top surface. Like the top arm of a binder clip. 11mm long, 3mm thick. Printed in PETG.

Spring / hinge (dark green arch)

The curved PETG wall that connects the upper jaw to the device body at the back. Its natural springiness (1.2mm wall thickness in PETG) keeps the jaws pressed together. No metal spring needed.

Device body = lower jaw (black)

The device body itself acts as the lower jaw. A thin clamping lip at the top presses against the book front cover from the front. The rest of the device hangs below — button, LED, and speaker all face the reader.

The key insight The jaw gap is the space between the upper jaw's bottom and the device body's top clamping face. The book's front cover board (~5mm) slides into this gap. PETG spring pressure holds it snugly. When the child clips it on, she just hooks the upper jaw tab over the top edge of the book — identical to clipping on a reading light.

Clip-on feels like...

The same motion as clipping a reading light onto a page or putting a chip clip on a bag. Hook the tab over the edge, let go. The PETG spring does the work.

For a 6-year-old: The child will hold the book with both hands. The device clips to the top-right corner. When she hits a word, her right thumb reaches the button without moving the book. She presses and holds, speaks the word, releases — Lexie responds.
Left-handed version: Clip to top-left corner instead. Same device, same clip — just mirrored orientation.

TinkerCAD tip Model the clip as a single solid shape: start with the device body box, then add the upper jaw box extending from the back-top edge, then add the curved spring connecting their rear faces. Use the "hole" tool to create the jaw gap. Export as STL and print the spring wall at 0.2mm layer height for best flex.
Test before ordering: Cut the clip profile from 1.5mm cardboard first. Clip it to the actual book your daughter is reading. Check the gap is right for that book's cover. Then adjust jaw gap in TinkerCAD before sending to print service.
4 — Child's view: device on book during reading
5 — Does Lexie block the text? (honest answer)

Yes, a little, if the body hangs on the page. With a top-edge clip and the device hanging down the front of the right page, the Lexie body covers a narrow vertical strip along the outer margin (roughly the width of the device: ~55–70 mm). Lines of text that run into that strip are partly hidden. The left page is clear. Section 4 above now shows that strip in light red so you can see exactly what is covered.

Ways to reduce obstruction

ApproachEffect
Narrower device widthThinner strip on page (trade: harder to fit electronics)
Clip as far right as possibleUses more of the printed margin; less overlap with body text on many books
Right-edge jaw (diagram B)Zero page overlap — entire body sits outside the page. Trade: device sticks out to the side; thumb reach is slightly different
L-bar (Section 6)Thin top bar + arm behind book — near-zero text obstruction; harder CAD / wire routing
Slide Lexie between usesClip only when a word comes up; rest of the time it sits on the table — not ideal for flow
Practical recommendation For v1, pick one: accept a ~55 mm margin strip on the right page, use a right-edge clip (zero overlap), or move to the L-bar layout in Section 6 — thin bar on top only; heavy parts behind the book.
6 — L-bar layout (thin top bar + arm behind book)

The L-bar splits Lexie into two limbs: a thin horizontal bar that sits along the top edge of the open book (clip + mic + small controls), and a vertical arm that folds behind the book (battery, XIAO, amp). The speaker lives on the inner face of that arm (the side facing the child) so sound fires toward the reader, not into the cover. The reading surface stays almost completely clear — only a shallow strip along the very top margin is “used” by the bar.

Tradeoffs vs. simple “bookmark slab” Two-part shell + elbow + wire routing through the hinge zone; print orientation matters (spring in bar vs. arm strength). You gain clear reading and a more “lamp-like” silhouette; you pay in CAD time and assembly. For a first print, consider a right-edge clip (simpler) before the full L-bar — or prototype L-bar in cardboard first.
